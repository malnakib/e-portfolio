Collaborative Discussion Forum Summaries

Discussion 1: Industry 4.0 to Industry 5.0

Units: 1 to 3

My Initial Post

I wrote about the CrowdStrike incident of July 2024, where a faulty update caused around 8.5 million Windows systems to crash worldwide. This led to flight cancellations, delays in hospital surgeries, and disruption to emergency services. I connected this to Metcalf's (2024) discussion of Industry 5.0, and argued that not giving users control over when updates are applied goes against the human-centric principles that the European Commission (2021) considers central to Industry 5.0. The main point I made was that systems should be built to recover from failure, rather than designed on the assumption that things will always work as expected.

Peer Responses I Wrote

To Monique Mizzi: She wrote about the HSE ransomware attack on Ireland's healthcare system. I agreed that AI should support professionals rather than replace them, and highlighted the legal and financial consequences of these types of failures. I also noted that Industry 5.0 alone does not solve the cybersecurity problem (Kour et al., 2024).

To Jonas Besier: He discussed the Cloudflare outage and how it affected AI chat systems. I agreed that users do not separate between frontend, backend, or network problems, they just see a broken product. I also raised the point that these kinds of outages can lead to lasting reputational damage, even when the root cause sits outside the company itself.

Key Learnings

This discussion made me think about how the systems I build at work could break at scale. It also helped me see Industry 5.0 not as an abstract idea, but as something with real design consequences. I now pay more attention to how much control users have, and whether the systems I work on can actually recover when something fails.

Discussion 2: AI Writing Tools

Units: 8 to 10

My Initial Post

After reading Hutson (2021), I shared where I think AI writing tools are useful and where they start to cause problems. I have used them at work for documentation and they work well for that kind of task. But for academic or research writing, the risks are higher because these models do not understand what they produce, they predict the next word based on patterns (Floridi and Chiriatti, 2020). I also discussed the effect on learning, since writing is part of how you work through ideas, and when AI handles that process, something important gets lost.

Peer Response

To Naeema Alnaqbi: I agreed with her point about AI systems being "stochastic parrots" (Bender et al., 2021). I shared examples from my own work where AI outputs looked polished but contained incorrect information. I also argued that rules alone will not solve this problem, people need to develop a better understanding of what these tools can and cannot do.

Summary Post

I wrapped up the discussion by pulling the main ideas together. AI writing tools are fine for everyday tasks, but for anything that needs real thinking, you have to do it yourself. Policies have their place, but in the end, what helps more is knowing where these tools stop being useful.

Team Project: Airbnb Business Analysis

Airbnb NYC 2019: Price Prediction and Market Segmentation

Team: Group A (Mosleh Al Nakib, Ebrahim Alkhateri, Mansour AlHamdani)

My Role: EDA and preprocessing, plus coordination, report writing, and final submission

Project Description

We worked with the Airbnb NYC 2019 dataset, which has 48,895 listings. The goal was to find out what affects listing prices and whether there are different market groups in the data. For price prediction we used Linear, Ridge, and Random Forest regression. For segmentation we used K-Means clustering.

My Contributions

  • Cleaned the dataset: handled missing values, removed outliers, applied log transformation
  • Created EDA visualisations: price distributions, borough/room type charts, geographic scatter plot
  • Set up the shared document and GitHub repository
  • Wrote the introduction and discussion sections of the report
  • Pulled all team members' work together for the final submission
  • Recorded and delivered the 20-minute presentation

Key Results

  • Random Forest achieved R² = 0.635, outperforming Linear and Ridge Regression (R² = 0.521)
  • K-Means with K=4 identified four market segments: Mid-Range Mixed ($121), High-Value Entire Homes ($203), Budget Private Rooms ($76), and Long-Stay Luxury Apartments ($273)
  • Location and room type were the strongest price predictors

Artefacts by Unit

Key artefacts and activities completed during the module.

Units 1 to 3: Discussion 1

I wrote an initial post about the CrowdStrike incident and how it relates to Industry 5.0. I also responded to two peers, one on the HSE ransomware attack and the other on the Cloudflare outage.

Unit 3: Correlation & Regression

I ran four notebooks on Pearson correlation, linear regression, multiple regression, and polynomial regression. I changed variables to see how noise and outliers affect the results. More noise made the correlation weaker, and setting the polynomial degree too high led to overfitting.

Unit 5: Jaccard Coefficient

I calculated Jaccard coefficients for three patient records. Jack and Mary were the most similar (0.67), and Jim and Mary the least (0.25). The interesting part is that Jaccard ignores matching negatives, because two patients both not having a symptom does not mean they are similar.

Unit 6: Project Proposal & Team Contract

We put together the initial project plan, including the business question and how we divided the work across EDA, regression, and clustering. The team contract was signed by all three members on 22 to 23 February 2026.

Unit 7: Perceptron Activities

I worked through three notebooks: a simple perceptron, the AND operator, and a multi-layer perceptron for XOR. A single perceptron could not handle XOR, but a multi-layer network solved it after 400,000 epochs. This gave me a better understanding of how weights control whether a neuron fires, and the trade-off between learning rate and training speed.

Unit 8: Gradient Cost Function

I tried different learning rates and iterations for gradient descent. With a learning rate of 0.5, the cost kept jumping and never settled. With 0.01, the cost went down but very slowly. A rate of 0.08 with 100 iterations worked best, bringing the cost from 89 down to 0.004.

Units 8 to 10: Discussion 2

Based on Hutson (2021), I explored where AI writing tools help and where they fall short. I shared my initial thoughts in the first post then responded to Naeema on the idea of "stochastic parrots" and finally wrapping up with a summary. The key point across all posts was that AI can handle routine writing, but a human should be involved when real thinking is needed.

Unit 9: CNN Object Recognition

I built a CNN using TensorFlow and Keras on the CIFAR-10 dataset, which has 60,000 images across 10 classes. The model reached about 63% test accuracy with early stopping after 6 epochs. Training accuracy got to around 70% while validation stayed at about 64%, which shows the model was starting to overfit. The model struggled most with telling cats and dogs apart.

Unit 11: Peer Evaluation & Presentation

Recorded a 20-minute presentation on the Airbnb project. Also completed the peer evaluation with a self-assessment score of 31/35. The biggest lesson from the project was that splitting tasks is not enough, you also need to agree on how work gets handed over.

Reflective Piece

Using the What, So What, Now What framework (Rolfe, Freshwater and Jasper, 2001).

The Module, The Project, and My Role

I started working on this module with little experience in machine learning. As a backend developer, I write code all the time, but building actual ML models was totally new to me. The module covered some aspects such as cleaning of the data, applying regression, and clustering. What helped most were the practical parts where we used Python and Scikit-Learn.

The main project was a team assignment. We were three team members in Group A, and we worked on the Airbnb NYC 2019 dataset. The goal was to predict listing prices using regression and to identify market groups using clustering. My part was EDA and data cleaning. I was also the only one who went to the tutor’s briefing about the assignment, so I shared what was covered and got the group moving on the work. From there, I set up the shared document and GitHub repo, wrote the introduction and discussion, and brought everything together for the final submission. Our main communication was through WhatsApp, but time zones made quick coordination hard.

I also joined two discussion forums. One was about Industry 5.0. I wrote about the outage of CrowdStrike and what it shows about relying too much on one system. The second discussion forum was about AI powered writing tools, where Hutson (2021) highlighted that these tools produce text without real understanding of it. I shared my own experience using them at work and where I think they stop being helpful, especially when accuracy matters and you cannot just trust what the model gives you.

Technical Growth and Teamwork Lessons

Going from reading about ML to actually using it was probably the biggest change for me. For example, I applied a log transformation to the price column, and it was interesting how the histogram started to shift from skewed to a normal shape. It was small changes, but help me to understand why the preprocessing matters.

What I did not see coming was how far ahead Random Forest would be. Both Linear and Ridge Regression gave R-squared of 0.52. Random Forest hit 0.63. I kept checking because I thought I had made a mistake, but when I thought about it, it made sense, the price is not just one thing. A private room in Manhattan is not the same as a private room in the Bronx, even if everything else matches. Géron (2022) says decision trees do not assume much about the data the way linear models do, and averaging across many trees in a random forest cuts down the variance a lot. Reading that in a book is one thing. Seeing a 10-point gap in your own numbers is different.

I did not write the clustering code, but working with the output and describing the clusters taught me more than I expected. For instance, the silhouette scores were around 0.26, which seemed low. At first I thought we did something wrong. Then I realised that in a city like New York, with so many different types of listings, overlap between groups is just how it is.

On the team side, I learned a hard lesson. Both teammates sent me their work as separate Word files instead of putting it into the shared document. So I ended up reformatting, and trying to make everything feel like one report instead of three separate pieces. I also was fixing the quietly instead of saying something. In my peer evaluation I said that next time, I would set clearer rules from the start about how work should be handed over. I still think that is the most important thing I learned from this project, not the ML part, but the process part.

The forums pushed me in a different direction. Writing about the CrowdStrike incident made me think about my own work and how a small mistake in a system I build could affect a lot of people. The AI writing discussion made me more careful about when I use these tools and when I should just write things myself.

Skills to Build and Habits to Change

After this module, I have a clearer picture of what I want to learn next. Random Forest worked well for our project, but I want to try XGBoost and see if gradient boosting can do better (Chen and Guestrin, 2016). I am also curious about DBSCAN for clustering, since K-Means forces every cluster into a round shape and that does not always match reality. Jain (2008) notes that data can reveal clusters of very different shapes and sizes, which makes algorithm choice important. Both of these could be useful at work. I deal with customer data and transaction patterns in fintech, and better models could help with things like fraud detection or customer grouping.

On the teamwork side, I know what I need to change. I have to be more direct at the start, agree on deadlines, agree on format, and not assume things will just work out. I also need to stop picking up other people’s slack without saying anything. It felt easier, but it is not something I want to keep doing. It is better to have one awkward conversation at the beginning than to be frustrated the whole way through.

This module also made me see that machine learning is not just about the accuracy score. The ethics discussions stayed with me more than I thought they would. Talking about system failures, AI-generated content, and who is actually responsible when something breaks, those conversations were harder to let go of. Mittelstadt et al. (2016) call this ‘de-responsibilisation’. People hide behind the system and assume the output must be right because a machine produced it. In fintech that is a real problem. The models I work with affect people’s money and their credit, and I cannot just shrug and say the system did it.

If I look at where I was when this module started and where I am now, the difference is clear. I came in knowing how to code but not how to build or evaluate ML models. I am leaving with a much better understanding of what works, what does not, and why the process around the work matters just as much as the technical part.
